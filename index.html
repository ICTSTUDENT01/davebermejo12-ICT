Variables in Quantitative Research: According to the Continuity of Values

Understanding Discrete and Continuous Variables

By Dave N. Bermejo 12 - ICT · July 2025 · Jose Maria Panganiban Senior High School PR-2 Blog

Have You Ever Measured Something Without Realizing It?

Think about it: how many siblings do you have? How tall are you right now? Even in everyday life, we're constantly using numbers — whether it's counting steps, measuring time, or checking our height. These numbers are examples of something researchers call variables.

In the world of quantitative research, variables are the foundation of any study. They are the values researchers measure, compare, and analyze to understand trends, behaviors, and relationships. But not all variables behave the same — some are fixed and countable, while others flow within a range.

In this blog, you’ll learn about the two major types of variables based on how their values behave: discrete and continuous. By the end, you’ll be able to tell them apart easily — and understand how they’re used in real research.

Discrete Variable

Description: A discrete variable is one that can only take whole number values. These values are fixed, distinct, and countable — such as the number of books read or the number of siblings a person has.

Characteristics:

  • Countable values
  • Whole numbers only (no decimals or fractions)
  • Values occur in gaps (e.g., 0, 1, 2...)
  • Often finite or limited range of outcomes

Usage & Purpose in Research: Discrete variables are useful in studies that involve exact counts or categories. They help researchers measure frequency, compare quantities, and identify patterns in groups or populations. For example, a researcher might study how the number of siblings affects student performance.

Data Collection/Methodology: These variables are usually collected through surveys, checklists, questionnaires, and simple observations — asking "How many?" rather than "How much?". They're often analyzed using frequency tables, bar graphs, and pie charts.

Continuous Variable

Description: A continuous variable is one that can take any value within a given range. These values are measurable, often include decimals or fractions, and can become more precise depending on how they're measured.

Characteristics:

  • Measurable values with infinite possibilities
  • Includes decimals and fractions
  • Can change gradually and precisely
  • No clear gaps between values

Usage & Purpose in Research: Continuous variables are used in research that involves detailed measurements — such as physical attributes, time intervals, or environmental data. They allow researchers to analyze trends, fluctuations, and averages over time or space.

Data Collection/Methodology: Continuous variables are gathered using measuring tools like rulers, thermometers, timers, or digital instruments. They're analyzed using statistical averages, histograms, line graphs, and scatter plots to observe detailed variations.

Conclusion

In quantitative research, recognizing the type of variable you're working with — whether discrete or continuous — is essential for accurate data collection, analysis, and interpretation. Discrete variables focus on exact counts, while continuous variables deal with precise measurements.

As a student or researcher, this knowledge helps you choose the right tools, ask the right questions, and represent your data correctly. Whether you’re counting people or measuring height — you’re using the power of variables to understand the world better.
